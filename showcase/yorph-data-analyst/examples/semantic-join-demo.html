What it had to figure out
Four kinds of mess, all mixed together.
The dataset was built to cover the ways people actually type product names — not just typos, but the deeper stuff that nothing based on letter-matching can solve.
~75% of rows
Abbreviations with no shared letters

Reps use initials and shorthand that share almost nothing in common with the full name. Letter-matching can't bridge this gap — it has to know what the short form means.

"HN Cheerios 18oz" → Cheerios Honey Nut 18.8oz
"Tylenol ES 100ct" → Tylenol Extra Strength 500mg 100ct
"OJ no pulp 52" → Tropicana Orange Juice No Pulp 52oz
~20% of rows
Descriptions instead of sizes

People describe products the way they remember them — "small can", "large box" — instead of looking up the exact size. The join has to map descriptive words to specific SKUs.

"Red Bull small can" → Red Bull Energy Drink 8.4oz
"frosted flakes lg box" → Frosted Flakes 24oz
~40% of rows
Brand names written many ways

Brand names get abbreviated, misspelled, or skipped entirely. The system has to recognize that different spellings refer to the same company's product.

"cliff bar choc chip 6pk" → Clif Bar Chocolate Chip 6ct
"smuckers straw jam 18oz" → Smucker's Strawberry Jam 18oz
~3% of rows
Genuinely unclear entries

Some entries can't be resolved for sure — and the system flags those honestly instead of guessing. Low-confidence rows go to human review, not the final total.

"diet cola 12pk" → Diet Coke or Diet Pepsi?
"cola 12 pack" → flagged · needs human review

How it works
Three stages. No per-row LLM calls.
The smart part — knowing that "HN" means "Honey Nut" — is figured out once, up front, from the catalog and a few samples. After that, every row goes through fast, cheap, deterministic code. It scales to millions of rows without breaking the budget.
Stage 1
Clean up the text

Expand abbreviations, fix casing, and apply domain-specific rules. "HN" becomes "Honey Nut", "lg box" becomes "large", "OJ" becomes "Orange Juice" — before any matching begins.

rules · regex · abbreviation dictionary
Stage 2
Find likely matches

Compare the cleaned-up entry against the catalog using word-level and character-level similarity. This narrows 60 possible matches down to the top few candidates — fast, without calling any AI model.

TF-IDF · word + character n-grams
Stage 3
Pick the winner — or flag it

Boost candidates that match on brand, size, or flavor signals. If one candidate clearly wins, accept it. If two are too close to call, flag the row for human review instead of guessing silently.

heuristic re-ranking · confidence scoring
